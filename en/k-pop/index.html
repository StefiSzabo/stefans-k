K-Pop culture refers to the popular music and lifestyle of South Korea, which has gained global recognition and influence in recent decades.
It encompasses not only the music itself, but also the fashion, beauty, dance, and fandom aspects of it.
The main features of the K-Pop culture are:

(If you meet terms that you don't know what they mean, you can find their definition after this)

1. The music

The K-Pop music is characterized by catchy hooks, polished choreography, grandiose live performances, and impeccably produced music videos.
It is influenced by various genres, such as Pop, Hip-Hop, R&B, Rap, Rock, EDM, as well as traditional Korean elements.
It often incorporates English words or phrases, have English versions of the songs, or have full English songs to appeal to international audiences.

2. The fashion

The K-Pop fashion is known for its colorful, trendy, and sometimes eccentric styles.
The idols often wear outfits that reflect their musical concepts or personalities, raging from cute and casual to chic and glamorous.
The K-Pop fashion also influences the street style and online shopping trends of many fans around the world.

3. The beauty

The K-Pop beauty is based on the ideal of having flawless skin, natural makeup, attractive features, and following the KBS.
K-Pop idols often endorse or collaborate with various cosmetic brands.
This beauty also inspires many fans to adopt skincare routines, try new products, or experiment with different looks.

4. The dance

The K-Pop dance is an integral part of the music and performance of K-Pop idols.
It involves complex and synchronized movements that showcase the skills and charisma of the idols.
It also attracts many fans who enjoy learning the choreography or creating their own covers of their favorite songs.

5. The fandom

The K-Pop fandom is one of the most passionate and loyal communities in the world.
The fans support their favorite idols by buying their albums and merchandise, streaming their songs and videos,
voting for them in awards shows, attending their concerts and fan meetings, and joining online fan clubs or forums.
The K-Pop fans also express their creativity and love for their idol by making fan art, fan fiction, fan videos, or fan projects.

The K-Pop culture is a phenomenon that reflects the diversity and dynamism of South Korea and its people.
It also connects millions of fans across different countries and cultures through a shared appreciation and enjoyment of music and entertainment.

K-Pop terms that you will hear a lot

1. K-Pop Stans

The word stan is a portmanteau of stalker and fan, and it originated from the 2000 song "Stan" by Eminem,
which tells the story of an obsessed fan who writes letters to his idol and eventually kills himself and his pregnant girlfriend.
The word stan has been since adopted by various fandoms to describe fans who are extremely devoted, passionate, and sometimes obsessive about their favorite celebrities or groups.
They are fans who follow and support one or more K-Pop artists or groups, often on social media platforms such as Twitter, Instagram or YouTube.
K-Pop Stans are known for their ability to mobilize and coordinate online campaigns, such as streaming songs and videos, voting for awards, trending hashtags, or sabotaging opponents.
They are also involved in various social and political causes, such as supporting the Black Lives matter movement, disrupting white supremacist hashtags, or pranking Donald Trump's rally.
However, some K-Pop stans may also engage in toxic or problematic behaviors, such as cyberbullying, harassment, or fan wars with other fandoms.

2. Kpoppers

The word kpopper is a combination of K-Pop and popper, which is a slang term for a person who likes pop music.
Kpoppers are fans who enjoy listening to K-Pop music and appreciate the diversity and quality of the genre.
They may not be as invested or involved in the fandom activities as K-Pop Stans, but they still respect and support the artists and groups they like.
Kpoppers may also be more open-minded and tolerant of different opinions and preferences than some K-Pop Stans.
They may like one or more K-Pop artists or groups, or they may like K-pop music in general.

3. The fandoms

The K-Pop fandoms are the communities of fans that support and love their favorite K-Pop artists or groups.
Each fandom has a name, color, and sometimes a symbol that represents their identity and loyalty.
They are known for their dedication, enthusiast, and creativity in showing their support for their idols.
They often buy albums and merchandise, stream songs and videos, vote for awards, attend concerts and fan meetings, and join online fan clubs or forums.
They also express their love and appreciation for their idols by making fan art, fan fiction, fan videos, or fan projects.

4. Light Sticks

Light Sticks are official devices that K-Pop fans use to cheer for their idols during concerts or events.
They are usually battery-operated and can change colors or patterns according to the music or the mood.
They also have a unique design that reflects the concept or the logo of the artist or group.
Light Sticks are not only used to create a beautiful and synchronized light show during performances, but also to symbolize the bond and unity between the idols and their fans.

5. The fancams

The K-Pop fancams are the videos that K-Pop fans record or upload of their idols during concerts or events.
They usually focus on one member or a specific moment that showcases their talent, charisma, or personality.
They are a way for fans to share their admiration and excitement for their idols with other fans or potential fans.
They can also be used to promote or defend their idols on social media platforms such as Twitter or Instagram.

6. K-Pop Idols

An K-Pop Idol is a celebrity which is produced by entertainment agencies and have undergone extensive training in dance, vocals, and foreign language.
They can be solo artists or members of a group, and they represent a hybrid of visuals, music, fashion, and dance.
They are expected to be exemplary people with artistic values and virtues, but they also make mistakes and face criticism.
(and sometimes so much crtisism that they just choose to leave this world...)
They are even forced by the agency to wear uncomfortable clothes if it looks the best on them, or if it fits the theme of the song.
The life of a K-Pop Idol can be challenging and rewarding at the same time.

Depending on their position and personality, K-Pop Idols may have different roles in their group or as solo artists.
Some of the common roles are leader, vocalist, rapper, dancer, visual and maknae (the youngest member).
Each role has its own responsibilities and expectations from the fans and the public.

K-Pop Idols are involved in various activities to promote their music and interact with their fans.
Some of the common activities are recording songs and albums, filming music videos and reality shows,
attending press conferences and interviews, performing on music shows and concerts, participating in fan events and social media platforms,
endorsing products and brands, and collaborating with other artists.

To become an K-Pop Idol there are some steps to follow.
The audition for one or more entertainment agencies, either in person or online.
It may involve singing, dancing, rapping, or acting skills.
The age range for auditioning can be from 10 to 20 years old.
If accepted, become a trainee under the agency and receive rigorous training in various aspects, such as vocal, dance, language, interview, and etiquette.
The training period can last from months to years, depending on the individual's progress and the agency's plans.
If selected, debut as a K-Pop Idol either as a solo artist or as a member of a group.
The debut may involve releasing a single or an album, appearing on music shows or variety shows, holding a showcase or a fan meeting, and performing live concerts.

7. The KBS

The Korean Beauty Standard, also named KBS, is a set of aesthetic preferences and expectations that are widely shared and promoted in South Korea and beyond.
It is influenced by various factors, such as historical, cultural, social, and media influences.
It is often considered to be strict and demanding, as it requires a high level of maintenance and conformity.
Here are some of the main aspects of it:

● Skin

The KBS values having fair, smooth, and flawless skin.
This is seen as a sign of health, youth, and purity.
Koreans spend a lot of time and money on skincare products and routines, such as the famous 10-step skincare regimen.
They also avoid sun exposure and use sunscreen, whitening creams, or even skin bleaching treatments to achieve a lighter complexion.

● Eyes

The KBS favors having large, round, and bright eyes.
This is seen as a sign of attractiveness, innocence, and charm.
Koreans often use makeup, such as eyeliner, eyeshadow, mascara, and false eyelashes, to enhance their eyes size and shape.
They also use contact lenses to change their eyes color or create a doll-like effect.
Many Koreans also undergo double eyelid surgery, which is the most common cosmetic procedure in South Korea, to create a crease in their upper eyelids.

● Face

The KBS emphasizes having a small, v-shaped face with a pointy nose, plump lips, and straight eyebrows.
This is seen as a sign of femininity, cuteness, and elegance.

● Body

Koreans often follow strict diets and exercise regimes to maintain or lose weight.
They also wear clothes that accentuate their body proportions or create an optical illusion of being taller or thinner.
Some Koreans also undergo body contouring surgeries, such as liposuction or breast augmentation, to improve their body shape or size.

The KBS is not only applied to women but also to men.
Some of the common features that are considered attractive for men are fair skin, double eyelids, high nose bridge, defined jawline, broad shoulders, muscular arms, and abs.
However, the KBS is not universally accepted or followed by all Koreans or foreigners.
Some people may have different preferences or opinions on what constitutes beauty.
Some people may also challenge or criticize the KBS for being unrealistic, oppressive, or harmful to one's physical and mental health.
Therefore, it is important to respect and appreciate the diversity and individuality of beauty in different cultures and societies.

More about K-Pop

1. K-Pop Generations

It is a way of categorizing the different eras and trends of Korean Pop music and culture.
According to the webzine IDOLOGY, there are four main generations of K-Pop, each with its own characteristics and influences.

● First Generation (1992 - 2002)

This generation marked the birth of K-Pop as a genre, with the debut of Seo Taiji and Boys, who mixed traditional Korean music with hip hop, rap, and rock.
Other first-generation groups includede H.O.T., S.E.S., and Fin.K.L, who focused on ballads and love songs for a young and female audience.
The first geenration also introduced the concept of idol groups, who were trained and produced by entertainment agencies.

● Second Generation (2003 - 2012)

This generation saw the rise of K-Pop Idol as a global phenomenon,
with groups like TVXQ, BoA, Super Junior, Girls' Generation, BIGBANG, 2NE1, and SHINee gaining popularity in Asia and beyond.
Their music was characterized by catchy beats, synchronized dance routines, and elaborate music videos.
The second generation also saw the emergence of the Korean Wave (or Hallyu), which spread Korean culture and entertainment to other parts of the world.

● Third Generation (2012 - 2019)

This generation witnessed a further expansion and diversification of the K-Pop industry, with more groups debuting and exploring different music styles and themes.
Groups like EXO, BTS, BLACKPINK, TWICE, SEVENTEEN, and Red Velvet became internationally succesful and influential, with their songs addressing topics such as mental health, self-love, and social issues.
The third generation also utilized social media platforms to connect with fans and promote their music.

● Fourth Generation (2019 - present)

This generation is still in its early stages, but it has shown a lot of potential and innovation. Groups like ITZY, TXT, ATEEZ, Stray Kids, (G)I-DLE, and ENHYPEN have debuted with their unique styles and sounds.
The fourth generation is expected to continue the legacy of K-Pop and bring new changes and challenges to the genre.

Entertainment companies

According to the latest survey, there were a total of 4.610 companies in South Korean entertainment industry as of 2020.
Among them, almost 2.800 were management agencies and 1.800 were production agencies.
However, the number of entertainment agencies may vary depending on the definition and scope of the industry.

The most successful and influential entertainment agencies in South Kore are often referred to as the Big 4, which consist of HYBE, JYP, SM and YG.
These four companies have produced some of the most popular and internationally acclaimed K-Pop groups.
As of February 2023, the market capitalization of these four companies ranged from 1.3 trillion to 6.9 trillion South Korean won.
